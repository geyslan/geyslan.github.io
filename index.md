I'm an Open Source Engineer at [Aqua Security](https://www.aquasec.com), working
on Linux **runtime security** with eBPF. Day to day I'm on
[Tracee](https://github.com/aquasecurity/tracee) (runtime security & forensics)
and the eBPF tooling around it -
[libbpfgo](https://github.com/aquasecurity/libbpfgo),
[BTFhub](https://github.com/aquasecurity/btfhub) - living at the
kernel/user-space boundary. These days the work is as much about *where* the
code should go - design, direction, the sharp edges others route around - as the
code itself.

I've been at this a long time, low-level by preference. Back in 2013 I started
poking at the Linux kernel purely out of curiosity - chasing bugs and small
improvements for the fun of it - which quietly turned into 70-plus authored
patches. Around then came a long dive into shellcode and x86 assembly, the odd
crackme, and eventually a peer-reviewed paper on shrinking shellcode without
changing its behavior that earned an honorable mention at SBSeg. A few of those
small tools have since been referenced in academic research and security
writeups - always a strange, nice surprise.

I spent years maintaining packages for [Arch Linux](https://archlinux.org)
(quieter on that front these days), enjoy C and Assembly more than is probably
healthy, write Go for a living, and keep an eye on
[Zig](https://ziglang.org) for where systems programming heads next. Comfortable
from NAND gates up to Go; happiest in the parts of the stack most people would
rather not look at.

> **"Talk is cheap. Show me the code."** - so [here's the
> code](https://github.com/geyslan).

Still an ordinary guy who introduces the occasional 🐛 and expects to be paid for
it. (Sometimes the bugs are free.) I once rambled about all this on the
[Hacking Bits](http://hackingbits.github.io) blog - long dormant now, but the old
posts still stand.
